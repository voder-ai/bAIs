## Summary

This paper evaluates five human-derived debiasing techniques (Devil's Advocate, SACD, Premortem, Outside View, Random Control) applied to LLMs across 22,773 anchoring bias trials. The core finding is that standard susceptibility metrics can mislead—the Devil's Advocate prompt looks best under susceptibility but worst under baseline proximity. The paper advocates reporting MAD alongside aggregate metrics.

---

## Strengths

1. **Important practical question.** Whether human cognitive debiasing techniques transfer to LLMs is highly relevant, and the paper provides useful cautionary evidence.

2. **Methodological transparency is exceptional.** The paper is remarkably honest about confounds (Outside View excluded, SACD turn-count confound, Haiku refusal rates, Opus zero variance), limitations, and the exploratory nature of the multi-domain study. This level of candor is rare and commendable.

3. **The metric divergence finding is genuinely useful.** Demonstrating that susceptibility and baseline proximity give opposite rankings (Table 4) is a concrete, actionable contribution for the field.

4. **Scale and reproducibility.** 22,773 trials across 10 models, with code/data availability, is substantial. The breadth of models tested is a strength.

5. **MAD recommendation is well-motivated.** The demonstration that SACD's 93.7% aggregate masks 18.1% MAD is compelling and the recommendation to report both metrics is sound.

---

## Major Issues

### 1. Fundamental Design Confound: One Prompt Per Technique

The paper tests **one prompt implementation per technique** and draws conclusions about whether the _techniques_ transfer. The abstract says "Do human debiasing techniques work on LLMs?" but the answer is really "Do these specific prompts work?" The paper acknowledges this repeatedly ("Alternative prompt formulations may perform differently") but the framing throughout—especially in the title, abstract, and conclusion—still overgeneralizes.

The Devil's Advocate finding is illustrative: the prompt asks the model to argue against its initial judgment, which predictably could anchor it further from baseline. A different DA implementation (e.g., "ignore the prosecutor's demand and argue from first principles") might perform entirely differently. **The paper's most dramatic finding (DA makes things worse) may be entirely an artifact of prompt design rather than a property of the Devil's Advocate technique.**

This is not a minor caveat—it undermines the paper's primary contribution. The paper should either: (a) test multiple prompt formulations per technique, or (b) reframe entirely as a study of specific prompts rather than techniques.

### 2. Proportional Anchor Design Creates Interpretive Challenges

Setting anchors at ±50% of each model's baseline is a reasonable choice but creates a non-standard experimental design that limits comparability with prior work. More importantly, the paper acknowledges but underexplores a key issue: the "12th offense" in the base vignette is itself a potential anchor. The "unanchored" baseline may not be truly unanchored. This is acknowledged in Limitations item 4 but deserves more analysis—if the baseline is itself anchored, the entire % of baseline metric is measuring deviation from an anchored state.

### 3. Mixed Effects Model with 10 Clusters

The paper fits a mixed-effects model with random intercepts for 10 models and appropriately cites Maas & Hox (2005) on minimum cluster sizes. However, the paper then reports specific fixed-effect estimates and p-values from this model. With 10 clusters, the random-effects variance is poorly estimated, which biases fixed-effect standard errors. The caveat "best treated as descriptive" is present but the paper still uses these results to "confirm" rankings. This is contradictory—either the model is confirmatory or it isn't.

### 4. Multi-Domain Study Is Underpowered and Uses Different Models

The multi-domain extension uses 5 models (vs. 10 in the main study), different model selection (adds Sonnet 4.5, drops 6 models), and shows that **no intervention outperforms debiasing in 3 of 6 domains**. While the paper frames this as supporting its "test per-task" message, it equally supports the interpretation that these prompts simply don't work and the main study's positive SACD result is domain-specific. The paper doesn't sufficiently grapple with this possibility.

### 5. Missing Critical Baselines

The paper acknowledges (Limitation 9) not testing simple "ignore the anchor" instructions or vanilla Chain-of-Thought. This is a serious omission. Without these controls, we cannot determine whether the observed effects come from the debiasing content or from simply instructing the model to be careful. The Random Control partially addresses turn count but not instruction content.

## Moderate Issues

### 6. GPT-5.2 Protocol Difference in Ablation

The 6-turn ablation (Section 5.5, Table 8) uses "single-prompt simulation" for GPT-5.2 vs. true multi-turn API for Anthropic models. GPT-5.2 shows 100% revision rate with 100% improvement—dramatically better than all other models. The caveat is boxed but this data point should probably be excluded from the ablation analysis entirely rather than presented as a finding.

### 7. Statistical Reporting Inconsistencies

- The ANOVA for temperature ($F(6, 8944) = 1.42$, $p = 0.203$) has 6 df in the numerator—this implies 7 groups, but 3 temperatures × 4 techniques = 12 groups (11 df for the interaction). The degrees of freedom don't match the stated interaction test.
- The power analysis states effective n ≈ 60-70 per technique with ICC=0.17, but the design effect calculation isn't shown. With ICC=0.17 and cluster size ~200-240 per model-technique, the design effect would be $1 + (240-1) \times 0.17 ≈ 41.6$, giving effective n ≈ 2200/41.6 ≈ 53. The stated 60-70 seems slightly optimistic.
- Cohen's d = 1.06 is mentioned for SACD vs. DA but never formally reported in a table with proper CIs.

### 8. Haiku Selection Bias

Haiku 4.5's 85%+ refusal rate means surviving trials are a heavily selected subsample. The paper includes these in analysis. A sensitivity analysis excluding Haiku is mentioned (shifts means <2pp) but the more concerning issue is that Haiku's extreme SACD result (47.8%) contributes to the "high variance" narrative that is central to the paper's message. If this data point is unreliable due to selection, the variance story is somewhat overstated.

### 9. Writing Quality

Generally clear and well-structured, but:

- The abstract is overloaded with caveats that, while honest, make the contributions hard to parse.
- Some information is repeated 3-4 times across abstract, intro, results, and conclusion (e.g., SACD's 48-128% range, DA's 63.6%).
- The paper could be 20-25% shorter without losing content.
- Section numbering in the multi-domain section references "Section 5" but the actual section numbering makes this Section 5 only with specific formatting.

## Verdict

The paper addresses an important question, is commendably transparent about limitations, presents internally consistent data, and makes a useful methodological contribution (MAD + metric divergence). However, the fundamental confound of testing one prompt per technique while drawing conclusions about techniques is a significant flaw that undermines the paper's primary claims. The missing baselines (simple "ignore anchor" instructions), the protocol inconsistency in the ablation study, the underpowered multi-domain extension with different model selection, and the 10-cluster mixed-effects model are additional concerns.

The paper is close to publishable quality but needs targeted revisions to bring claims in line with evidence. The metric divergence finding and MAD recommendation alone are valuable contributions if properly scoped.

**NEEDS REVISION**

**Key required changes:**

1. **Reframe from "techniques" to "prompts"** throughout, or test multiple prompt formulations per technique. The current framing overgeneralizes.
2. **Add simple instruction baselines** ("ignore the anchor," basic CoT) to establish a meaningful lower bound.
3. **Remove or heavily caveat the GPT-5.2 ablation data** given the protocol difference.
4. **Either remove the mixed-effects confirmatory language or increase cluster count.** With 10 clusters, present it purely as descriptive.
5. **Address the multi-domain divergence more directly:** if no intervention beats debiasing in 3/6 domains, the paper needs to seriously consider whether these prompts work at all, rather than framing this as supporting "test per-task."
