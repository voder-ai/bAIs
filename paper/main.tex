\begin{abstract}
Do human debiasing techniques work on LLMs? We test one prompt implementation per technique for four approaches from the cognitive bias literature---Devil's Advocate, SACD, Premortem, Outside View---plus a Random Control---across two studies totaling 22,773 anchoring bias trials (14,152 main study + 8,621 multi-domain extension).

\textbf{For these prompts: not reliably.} Some prompts help (our SACD prompt achieves 93.7\% of unanchored baseline, though confounded by its 6-turn structure vs.\ 3 turns for others), but one makes things worse: our Devil's Advocate prompt produces responses at only 63.6\% of baseline---\textit{further from baseline than doing nothing} (72.9\%). Worse, this prompt looks best under the standard metric (susceptibility), which measures consistency rather than accuracy.

Effectiveness varies dramatically: our SACD prompt shows the highest model variance, ranging from 48\% to 128\% of baseline (driven by Haiku's severe undershoot and Kimi's overshoot; see Table 5). Point-estimate rankings vary across domains (CIs overlap). No prompt consistently outperforms across all models.

\textbf{Methodological recommendation:} We advocate reporting \textbf{MAD} (Mean Absolute Deviation from baseline) alongside aggregate metrics. Aggregate \% of baseline can mislead: SACD's 93.7\% masks 18.1\% MAD because over/undershoots cancel.

\textbf{For practitioners:} Don't assume a single prompt implementation transfers. Test per-model, per-task. Report MAD alongside aggregates.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%==============================================================================

Human debiasing techniques---Devil's Advocate, Premortem, deliberative reasoning---are increasingly applied to LLMs. But do specific prompts implementing these techniques actually work? We test one prompt per technique across 22,773 trials and find transfer is not straightforward: our SACD prompt's effectiveness ranges from 48\% to 128\% of baseline across models. Different prompt formulations may yield different results. Most concerning, standard evaluation metrics can recommend the wrong prompt.

\subsection{Two Metrics, Two Rankings}
\label{sec:two-metrics}

Prior work evaluates debiasing using \textbf{susceptibility}---the gap between high-anchor and low-anchor responses:
\begin{equation}
\text{Susceptibility} = |\bar{R}_{high} - \bar{R}_{low}|
\end{equation}
Lower gap = less susceptible. But susceptibility measures \textit{consistency}, not \textit{accuracy}. A prompt can achieve low susceptibility while landing far from the unanchored judgment.

\textbf{Our approach:} We add \textbf{\% of baseline}---proximity to the model's unanchored judgment:
\begin{equation}
\text{\% of Baseline} = \frac{R_{prompt}}{R_{baseline}} \times 100\%
\end{equation}
100\% = the model's anchor-free response (which may itself be incorrect).

\subsection{Contributions}

\textit{Turn-count caveat:} SACD uses $\sim$6 turns vs.\ 3 for others; this confounds comparisons.

(1) \textbf{Aggregate metrics mask bidirectional error.} We demonstrate that aggregate \% of baseline can mislead: SACD achieves 93.7\% but 18.1\% MAD, because over/undershoots cancel. We recommend reporting MAD alongside aggregates.
(2) \textbf{Main study:} Deep validation (14,152 judicial trials, 10 models) showing substantial model-specific variance (SACD: 48\%--128\%).
(3) \textbf{Multi-domain extension:} Cross-domain evaluation (8,621 trials, 6 domains, 5 models) showing prompt rankings vary by domain and metric. Combined total: 22,773 trials.

\section{Related Work}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Anchoring Bias in Human Judgment}

Anchoring bias---the disproportionate influence of initial information on subsequent estimates---is among the most robust findings in cognitive psychology \citep{tversky1974}. Even experts are susceptible: \citet{englich2006} demonstrated that experienced judges' sentencing decisions were influenced by random numbers generated by dice rolls. Effect sizes of $d = 0.6$--$1.2$ persist regardless of anchor source or participant awareness. Our experimental paradigm adapts this judicial sentencing design.

\subsection{Cognitive Biases in LLMs}

LLMs exhibit human-like cognitive biases \citep{binz2023,jones2022,chen2025cognitive}, including anchoring \citep{huang2025anchoring,song2026reasoning}. Unlike humans, LLMs enable exhaustive systematic testing.

\subsection{Debiasing Techniques}

We test one prompt per approach for five categories: \textbf{SACD} (iterative self-correction \citep{lyu2025}), \textbf{Devil's Advocate} (argue against initial response), \textbf{Premortem} (imagine decision failed \citep{klein2007}), \textbf{Random Control} (neutral filler to isolate turn-count effects), and \textbf{Outside View} (reference class forecasting \citep{kahneman1993,sibony2019}, excluded from rankings due to confound---see Section~\ref{sec:outside-view-confound}). Recent work on framing debiasing \citep{lim2026deframe} shares conceptual overlap.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology}

Standard anchoring evaluation uses \textbf{susceptibility} (high--low spread). We add \textbf{\% of baseline} (proximity to unanchored judgment). Unlike the classic Anchoring Index \citep{jacowitz1995}, which uses baseline in both numerator and denominator, our metric avoids circularity. Low Anchoring Index with poor baseline proximity indicates consistent but wrong responses---the failure mode we observe with our DA implementation. We use free-form numeric responses rather than MCQs to avoid position and label biases documented in benchmark evaluations \citep{nowak2026abcd}.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:methodology-metrics}

We compare susceptibility (standard) with \% of baseline (Section~\ref{sec:two-metrics}). Susceptibility = high-low spread; \% of baseline = proximity to unanchored judgment.

\textbf{Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD):} We define MAD as the average per-trial deviation from the model's unanchored baseline:
\begin{equation}
\text{MAD} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left| \frac{r_i}{b_m} - 1 \right| \times 100\%
\end{equation}
where $r_i$ is the response for trial $i$, $b_m$ is model $m$'s unanchored baseline response, and the sum is over all anchored trials. Lower MAD indicates responses closer to unanchored judgment. Unlike aggregate \% of baseline, MAD does not allow positive and negative deviations to cancel.

\textbf{Why both?} Metrics can give divergent rankings---reducing spread may move responses away from baseline (Section~\ref{sec:metric-divergence}).

\textbf{Baseline as reference, not ground truth.} Following \citet{jacowitz1995}, we measure deviation from each model's unanchored baseline rather than external ground truth. This operationalizes debiasing as \textit{consistency}: similar outputs regardless of anchors. Baselines are not normatively correct (models disagree substantially, Table~\ref{tab:baselines})---we measure anchor-independence, not correctness.

\textbf{Proportional anchors.} Anchors set at $\pm$50\% of each model's baseline ensure comparable pressure across models. Not circular: baselines measured independently, then used for anchors. 100\% = anchor-independent judgment.

\subsubsection{Statistical Analysis}

Welch's t-test with Bonferroni correction (6 comparisons, $\alpha = 0.0083$) for hypothesis testing; percentile bootstrap (10,000 resamples, stratified by model) for CIs. Cohen's $d$ for effect sizes. Aggregates are trial-weighted means; see Table~\ref{tab:sacd-by-model} for model-level. Trials collected via OpenRouter API (February 2026); full details in repository.

\textbf{Power analysis:} Model clustering (ICC=0.17) reduces effective $n$ to $\approx$60--70 per prompt; powered to detect $d \geq 0.50$. Large effects ($d = 1.06$ SACD vs.\ DA) are reliably detectable; SACD--Premortem ($d = 0.08$) is underpowered.

\subsection{Confounds and Limitations}

\textbf{Edge models:} Opus 4.6 shows zero baseline variance (always 18mo); Haiku 4.5 has 85\%+ refusal rates (surviving trials may exhibit selection bias). \textbf{Sensitivity analysis:} Excluding Opus shifts means $<$3pp; excluding Haiku shifts means $<$2pp. Rankings preserved in both cases: SACD remains \#1 (91.2\% excl.\ Haiku), Premortem \#2, DA \#4.

\textbf{Outside View:} Required jurisdiction specification, introducing potential secondary anchor. Excluded from rankings (Section~\ref{sec:outside-view-confound}).

\textbf{Why the divergence?} Our DA implementation produces \textit{consistent} responses (low spread) that remain \textit{far from baseline}. SACD produces \textit{variable} responses (higher spread) that are \textit{closer to baseline on average}---though the average masks bidirectional deviation (Table~\ref{tab:anchor-asymmetry}).

\textbf{Magnitude of improvement:} Without any debiasing, anchored responses reach 72.9\% of baseline---a deficit of 27.1 percentage points from the unanchored judgment. SACD achieves 93.7\%, reducing this deficit to 6.3pp. This represents a reduction of 20.8pp, though the residual deficit and bidirectional variance (MAD=18.1\%) remain limitations.

\textbf{Effect sizes:} With model clustering (ICC=0.17, design effect $\approx$35), trial-level Cohen's $d$ would be inflated. We therefore report effect sizes via mixed-effects modeling (Section~\ref{sec:mixed-effects}), which properly accounts for the nested structure. The SACD--DA difference is large and robust; SACD--Premortem is negligible.

\subsection{High-Anchor Responses (No Technique)}

Under high-anchor conditions without intervention, two distinct response patterns emerge:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Compression}: Response pulled \textit{below} baseline (Anthropic models, GPT-4.1)
    \item \textbf{Inflation}: Response pulled above baseline (GPT-5.2, GLM-5, o3)
\end{enumerate}

The compression pattern is counterintuitive---high anchors typically pull responses upward. We hypothesize this reflects \textbf{anchor rejection}: some models recognize the high prosecutor demand as unreasonable and overcorrect downward. This is consistent with research showing that implausible anchors can trigger contrast effects rather than assimilation \citep{tversky1974}. 

\textbf{Which models compress?} Anthropic models (Opus, Sonnet, Haiku) and GPT-4.1 consistently show compression under high anchors. OpenAI's reasoning models (o3, o4-mini) and GPT-5.2 show the expected inflation pattern. This model-family clustering suggests compression may relate to training methodology or safety tuning rather than model scale.

\textbf{Implications:} The compression pattern does not invalidate our \% of baseline metric---in fact, it highlights its value. For compression models, a prompt that \textit{increases} responses toward 100\% is improving, even though it moves responses ``upward.'' Our metric captures this correctly: 90\% of baseline is better than 70\% of baseline, regardless of direction.

\subsection{Mixed Effects Analysis}
\label{sec:mixed-effects}

To account for non-independence of observations within models, we fit a linear mixed effects model:

\begin{equation}
y_{ijk} = \beta_0 + \beta_{\text{technique}} + \beta_{\text{anchor}} + \beta_{\text{technique} \times \text{anchor}} + u_j + \epsilon_{ijk}
\end{equation}

where $y_{ijk}$ is the \% of baseline for trial $i$ in model $j$ under anchor direction $k$ (high/low), $\beta_{\text{technique}}$ is the fixed effect for technique, $\beta_{\text{anchor}}$ captures the main effect of anchor direction, $u_j \sim N(0, \sigma^2_u)$ is the random intercept for model $j$, and $\epsilon_{ijk}$ is the residual error. Analysis includes 8,956 trials across 10 models and 4 techniques (excluding Outside View due to confound). The anchor direction effect is substantial: high-anchor trials average +14.5 pp above low-anchor trials across all techniques, consistent with the asymmetry reported in Table~\ref{tab:anchor-asymmetry}.

ICC = 0.17, indicating 17\% of variance is attributable to model differences. Technique $\times$ anchor interaction: $F(3, 8950) = 47.3$. \textbf{Cluster caveat:} With only 10 model clusters, denominator df are approximate (likely inflated) and random-effects variance estimates may be unreliable \citep{maas2005}; treat as descriptive. Fixed effects suggest ranking: SACD $+11.9$ pp (percentage points), Premortem $+9.8$ pp, Random Control $-3.5$ pp, Devil's Advocate $-18.2$ pp (relative to grand mean 81.8\%).

\textbf{Random slopes:} SACD shows highest slope variance (SD = 25.6 pp), showing effectiveness varies dramatically across models (+33\% to $-$56\% from fixed effect). This justifies per-model testing (Table~\ref{tab:sacd-by-model}).

\subsection{The SACD vs.\ Premortem Tradeoff}

SACD (93.7\%, CI [92, 95]) and Premortem (91.6\%, CI [90, 93]) are statistically equivalent (TOST $p < 0.01$, equivalence bound $\pm$5pp---approximately 1.5 months on a 30-month baseline, below the precision relevant to deployment). However, SACD's aggregate masks 18.1\% MAD vs.\ Premortem's 22.6\%. Model-specific variation dominates; per-model testing is essential.

\subsection{Key Findings}

(1) Rankings depend on metric and domain; all \#1 vs \#2 CIs overlap---practical equivalence.
(2) SACD's ranking varies from \#2 to \#5 across domains despite \#1 in main judicial study.
(3) \textbf{No intervention outperforms debiasing prompts in 3 of 6 domains} (Judicial-DUI, Judicial-Fraud, Aggravated-Theft by MAD). Debiasing can increase per-trial error.
(4) Fraud domain shows severe anchoring (29--75\% of baseline across prompts).

\textbf{Why do studies diverge?} The main study uses \% of baseline (10 models, single vignette); multi-domain uses MAD (5 models, 6 vignettes). Three factors explain the discrepancy: (a) \textit{Metric choice}---SACD's bidirectional variance penalizes it under MAD but cancels under aggregate \%. (b) \textit{Model selection}---multi-domain excludes 5 models from the main study. (c) \textit{Domain effects}---judicial sentencing may be uniquely suited to iterative reflection; loan/medical domains show different patterns. These findings reinforce our core message: prompt rankings are metric- and domain-dependent.

\textbf{Implications:} No prompt transfers reliably; test per-task, per-model.

%==============================================================================
\section{Discussion}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Why SACD Works (and Fails)}

SACD achieves highest \% of baseline but also highest variance (Table~\ref{tab:sacd-by-model}). Possible mechanisms: (1) iterative reflection helps escape local optima; (2) some models perform ``debiasing theater'' (Opus overshoots to 127.8\%); (3) Haiku's severe undershoot (47.8\%) shows SACD can backfire. Recent work supports these patterns: \citet{llm-bayesian-2025} find LLMs are ``Bayesian in expectation, not realization,'' and \citet{llm-judge-overconfidence-2025} demonstrate overconfident self-judgment.

\subsection{Why Random Control Works}

Random Control outperforms our DA implementation (+15pp) despite no debiasing content. One hypothesis: explicit bias prompts may prime models to attend to anchors they would otherwise ignore (iatrogenic effect). Random Control's neutral filler avoids this priming while providing multi-turn structure.

\subsection{Design Confounds}
\label{sec:outside-view-confound}

\textbf{Outside View:} Required jurisdiction specification (``German federal courts'') to avoid safety refusals, introducing a secondary anchor toward German norms. Excluded from rankings.

\textbf{Anchor strength:} We used $\pm$50\% proportional anchors. Initial $\pm$40\% anchors showed no effect on some models; some prior claims of LLM immunity to anchoring may reflect weak anchor design.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}

Our judicial sentencing vignettes are synthetic research stimuli, not decision-support tools. However, we acknowledge that LLMs are increasingly deployed in contexts where anchoring bias has real consequences: bail recommendations, loan approvals, medical triage. Our finding that standard evaluation metrics can recommend prompts that make bias \textit{worse} has direct implications: practitioners should not assume that published ``debiasing'' prompts transfer safely. The large model variance we observe (SACD: 48\%--128\%) means a prompt validated on one model may fail---or backfire---on another. We recommend domain-specific validation before any high-stakes deployment, and caution against using our results to justify LLM use in consequential decisions without such validation.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Vignette coverage.} Original: one judicial case. Extended with 3 additional vignettes (DUI, fraud, theft); rankings vary (Table~\ref{tab:vignette-comparison}).
    
    \item \textbf{Proportional anchor design.} Anchors scale with baseline ($\pm$50\%). Cross-model susceptibility comparisons are not valid; all claims are within-model only. Aggregates mask per-trial variance. Future work: validate with fixed absolute anchors.
    
    \item \textbf{Outside View confound.} Reference class prompts required jurisdiction specification, introducing secondary anchors. Core findings hold excluding Outside View. See Section~\ref{sec:outside-view-confound}.
    
    \item \textbf{Baseline interpretation.} Baseline includes ``12th offense''---a possible implicit anchor per the original \citet{englich2006} design. Our ``unanchored'' condition removes the prosecutor's demand, not all numeric context. 100\% = restored to demand-absent state, not ``correct.''
    
    
    \item \textbf{Model coverage.} 10 models from 5 providers. Excluding Opus (zero baseline variance) shifts means 2--3pp but preserves rankings.
    
    \item \textbf{Multi-domain coverage.} Section 5 uses 5 models; results are exploratory with overlapping CIs.
    
    \item \textbf{GPT-5.2 protocol.} GPT-5.2 used single-prompt simulation (full conversation in one call) vs.\ true multi-turn API for Anthropic models. GPT-5.2 ablation patterns may reflect protocol differences; Anthropic results are more reliable.
    
    
    \item \textbf{Turn-count confound (partially addressed).} SACD uses $\sim$6 turns vs.\ 3 for others. Multi-turn structure helps (Random Control +5pp over single-turn), but our 6-turn ablation (n=238) shows SACD's specific content explains additional variance beyond turn count---especially for models less prone to implicit revision.
    
    \item \textbf{Stopping rule.} Targeted $n \geq 30$ per condition as minimum sample size. Bootstrap CIs provide valid inference; effect sizes ($d > 0.5$) suggest adequate power.
    
    \item \textbf{Missing baselines.} We did not test simple ``ignore the anchor'' instructions or Chain-of-Thought without debiasing content. These minimal interventions might outperform complex prompts; future work should establish this lower bound.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Practical Recommendations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Add conversation turns.} Multi-turn structure (Random Control) improves +5pp over single-turn baseline (78.3\% vs 72.9\%) and outperforms our DA implementation by +15pp.
    \item \textbf{Test per-model.} SACD ranges 48\%--128\% of baseline across models.
    \item \textbf{Collect baselines.} Susceptibility alone misses failure modes like our DA implementation's.
    \item \textbf{Avoid reference class prompts} without careful design (Section~\ref{sec:outside-view-confound}).
    \item \textbf{Consider cost.} Premortem matches SACD in three turns vs.\ six.
\end{enumerate}

%==============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%==============================================================================

Our implementations of human debiasing prompts show mixed transfer to LLMs. Our 22,773-trial evaluation finds: our Devil's Advocate prompt makes bias \textit{worse} (63.6\% of baseline vs 72.9\% without intervention). Our SACD and Premortem prompts help on average (91--94\%), but SACD ranges from 48\% to 128\% across models. Point-estimate rankings vary across domains (CIs overlap); no prompt consistently outperforms.

\textbf{For practitioners:} Don't assume human techniques transfer to LLM prompts. Test per-model, per-domain. Report baseline proximity alongside susceptibility---a prompt can look good on standard metrics while making responses worse.

\textbf{For researchers:} Collect unanchored baselines. Susceptibility alone missed our DA implementation's failure.

\section{Prompt Templates}
\label{app:prompts}

Complete prompts are available in our repository. We summarize the key structure here.

\textbf{Base vignette (judicial):} Defendant convicted of shoplifting (12th offense). Includes case details, expert opinion, and pre-sentencing report. For anchored conditions, adds: ``The prosecutor demands [ANCHOR] months.''

\textbf{Technique prompts (exact wording):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Devil's Advocate (Turn 2):} ``Before finalizing your recommendation, consider: What would someone who disagrees with your initial judgment say? What are the strongest arguments against the sentence you're considering? After considering these counterarguments, provide your final sentencing recommendation.''
    \item \textbf{Premortem (Turn 2):} ``Before finalizing, imagine: It's one year from now, and your sentencing recommendation was found to be seriously flawed---perhaps overturned on appeal or widely criticized. What went wrong? What factors might you have overlooked or misjudged? After this analysis, provide your final recommendation.''
    \item \textbf{SACD (6-turn):} Iterative self-reflection following \citet{lyu2025}. Turn 2: ``Before answering, consider whether your judgment might be influenced by irrelevant information in the case description.'' Turns 3--5: Explicit bias acknowledgment prompts. Turn 6: ``Now provide a revised sentencing recommendation.''
    \item \textbf{Random Control (3-turn):} ``To ensure thorough consideration, please describe the courtroom setting you envision for this case.'' (Turn 2) + ``Thank you. Now provide your sentencing recommendation.'' (Turn 3)
    \item \textbf{Outside View:} ``What sentence do defendants with similar profiles typically receive in German federal courts?'' (confounded by jurisdiction anchor; excluded from rankings)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Multi-domain vignettes:} Loan approval (first-time homebuyer), medical triage (hours to treatment), salary negotiation, plus three judicial variants (DUI repeat, tax fraud first-time, aggravated theft 4th offense). Full vignette text and response distributions: \url{https://github.com/voder-ai/bAIs}
